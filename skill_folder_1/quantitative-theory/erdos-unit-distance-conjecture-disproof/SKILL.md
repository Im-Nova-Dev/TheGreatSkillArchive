# Disproof of the Erdős Unit Distance Conjecture

## 1. The Problem

**Erdős unit distance conjecture (1946).** Let u(n) denote the maximum number of pairs of
points at distance exactly 1 among any n points in the plane. Erdős conjectured

    u(n) = n^{1+o(1)}.

He also offered a $500 prize.

## 2. Prior Landscape (Before 2026)

- Trivial lower bound: ~2n from placing points on a line at distance 1.
- Square-grid construction gives at most n^{1+o(1)} unit distances, with the best constants
  coming from rational-distance lattice points.
- Most experts expected u(n) to grow like n·^{1+δ} with δ extremely small, or perhaps
  just O(n^{1+ε}) for any ε>0 — but not polynomial with a fixed positive exponent.

The widely accepted belief: **no fixed δ>0 exists.**

## 3. The Disproof (2025–2026, OpenAI model + arXiv:2605.20695, 2605.20579)

An OpenAI general-purpose reasoning model constructed a point set with

    u(n) ≫ n^{1+δ}    for some δ > 0    (later δ ≈ 0.014 by refinement)

permanently refuting the conjecture.

### 3.1 Key Technical Ingredients

- **Algebraic number theory method.** The proof deliberately stepped outside combinatorial
  geometry into number theory, using:
  - *Split prime principles.* Counts of primes splitting over quadratic extensions give
    control over how many solutions a congruence system has.
  - *Pigeonhole arguments.* Prime splitting guarantees many small solutions simultaneously.
  - *Golod-Shafarevich theorem (1964).* An inequality on the dimension of graded algebras gives
    a lower bound on the number of points realizing many unit distances.
  - *Modifications of Hajir-Maire-Ramakrishna (2021).* Earlier constructions of many points
    on algebraic curves were adapted.
- **Non-explicit configuration.** The smallest explicit realization would require about
  10^{1,000,000} points; the existence proof is non-constructive.

### 3.2 Result

There exist point sets in the plane with Ω(n^{1+δ}) unit distances for a fixed δ > 0.
Consequently:

    lim sup_{n→∞} u(n) / n^{1+ε} = ∞   for every ε > 0.

## 4. Verification & Reception

- Hand-verified by Timothy Gowers ("quite digestible", "novel and exciting") and Terence Tao.
- Formal verification in a proof assistant (Lean) has *not* been completed, though
  the technique map is being discussed as a target.
- The 28-page arXiv companion paper (2604.06609) also contains AI-generated short proofs
  of five other Erdős problems covering combinatorics, probability, and number theory.

## 5. AI-Assisted Proof Significance

- First major new mathematics delivered by a non-specialized, closed-form LLM reasoning
  model without a math-specific harness.
- Succeeded in ~50% of attempts (not cherry-picked out of countless failures).
- Cross-domain tool transfer: algebraic number theory applied to discrete geometry.
- Prompted debate: understanding-vs-results, attribution in AI-derived proofs, goalpost
  shifts ("PhD-level intelligence"), and the sustainability of human mathematical
  comprehension as AI accelerates output rates.

## 6. Teaching Exercises

1. **Density comparison.** For the m × m integer grid, each point has at most O(m) neighbors
   at distance 1 (these come from integer solutions to a² + b² = k² for small k). Show by
   counting that this gives n^{1+o(1)} on n-point grids — but never Ω(n^{1+δ}).
2. **Prime splitting recap.** Let K = Q(√d). State the condition on ramified vs. split primes
   in K using Legendre/Kronecker symbols. Explain why many split primes give many linear
   congruence solutions simultaneously.
3. **Golod-Shafarevich sketch.** For a graded algebra generated by an r-degree set with
   relations of degree > 2d, the inequality dim A_n ≥ (r-1)d·n + 1 shows growth. Explain
   briefly how ensuring many initial solutions (from number theory) bootstraps this growth.
4. **AI limits exercise.** Formalize why non-constructivity is not a flaw here. Give two
  advantages of non-constructive existence proofs in combinatorics (e.g., Ramsey theory).
5. **Cross-domain transfer.** Find an analogous case: a classical number theory technique
  applied to a graph theory or combinatorial geometry problem in the literature.
