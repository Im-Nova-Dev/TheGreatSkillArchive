# Two-Dimensional Metals and Van der Waals Squeezing

## First-Principles Concept
A two-dimensional (2D) material has charge carriers confined in one dimension so strongly that it behaves like an electron gas in an ultra-thin sheet. For semiconductors and layered van der Waals (vdW) materials such as graphene, this occurs naturally because weaker interlayer forces allow exfoliation. Metals resist 2D formation because their electrons are delocalized over a dense 3D bond network.

## Why 2D Metals Seemed Impossible
Metallic bonding relies on non-directional electron sharing across all axes. Down to a monolayer, that same bond network would normally force the sheet to crumple or re-stick unless the surfaces are both flat and chemically inert.

## vdW Squeezing Synthesis
1. Select metal powder and melt between monolayer MoS₂ anvils on sapphire.
2. Cooling under ~200 MPa forces the liquid into a flat, confined geometry.
3. The inert MoS₂ surfaces avoid bonding, while their high stiffness transmits pressure without rupture.
4. Result: angstrom-scale metal sheets.

Pressure compensates for the tendency of metal atoms to contract into bulk-like structures, while the vdW surfaces prevent lateral adhesion.

## Possible New Physics
- **Quantum confinement:** In 2D metals, the motion perpendicular to the sheet is quantized. That changes density of states and can alter conductivity, electron–phonon coupling, and eventually superconductivity.
- **Fermi surface engineering:** A 2D Fermi surface may produce anisotropic transport, opening paths for ultra-high-frequency devices.
- **Topological phase transitions:** In cooperation with strong spin–orbit metals such as bismuth or lead, thickness control may tune band topology.

## Distinction from Graphene-Like 2D Materials
Graphene is a zero-gap semimetal with linear dispersion (Dirac cones), while 2D metals retain parabolic bands with finite density of states at the Fermi level, behaving more like a conventional metal sheet than a semiconductor.

## Teaching Exercises
1. Explain why graphene exfoliates easily whereas copper does not, even though both are mechanically strong, using electron localization and bonding directionality.
2. Sketch the density of states near the Fermi level for a bulk metal versus a truly 2D metal and predict how conductivity might change.
3. For a vdW squeezing experiment, identify two mechanical requirements for the anvil materials and explain what fails if the substrate is not stiff enough.
4. Research sketch: propose an experiment to test whether the 2D Sn sheet becomes superconducting below some temperature.
