# Teaching Molecular Hydrogen Superfluidity

Use this skill when explaining the 2025 *Science Advances* result by Momose et al. on superfluid p-H₂ clusters.

## What to cover

1. **Bulk paradox**: Hydrogen freezes at 13.8 K, above its predicted superfluid transition (~1–2 K). Therefore bulk H₂ was assumed unable to show superfluidity.
2. **Finite-size escape route**: Helium nanodroplets keep H₂ in a liquid-like cluster below the freezing point, creating a matrix where superfluid order can emerge.
3. **Probe choice**: Methane is ideal because its interaction with p-H₂ is weak (93 cm⁻¹), so it minimally disturbs the hydrogen environment. The ν₄ bending mode gives narrow 65 MHz lines, enabling precise rotation measurements.
4. **Sticking factor κ**: Define κ as the fraction of the hydrogen shell that rotates with methane. κ → 0 correlates with superfluid decoupling.
5. **Bosonic exchange criterion**: Superfluidity in bosonic clusters is signaled by >60% of molecules participating in quantum exchanges for N ≥ 5.
6. **Path-integral Monte Carlo**: Show how simulation results for cluster superfluid fraction match the experimental κ trend.
7. **Andronikashvili experiment connection**: The 1946 Andronikashvili experiment measured the normal fluid fraction in superfluid helium using rotating disks. Here, the spinning methane molecule plays the role of the disk — a nanoscale rotor whose friction reveals the superfluid fraction. This historical analogy makes the modern experiment immediately intuitive.
8. **Cluster size threshold**: Present the sharp transition: N < 6 (normal fluid), N ≈ 10 (onset), N > 10 (perfect superfluid). Emphasize that N = 10–15 molecules is enough for a robust superfluid fraction.

## Plain-language summary

> Hydrogen is the simplest molecule. For decades physicists thought it should become a superfluid around 1 K, but it turns solid at 13.8 K, which seemed to block the effect. By trapping tiny hydrogen clusters inside liquid helium droplets, researchers cooled them into a state where most molecules move together in a quantum dance with zero friction — a superfluid. An embedded methane molecule told the story by how freely it could spin inside the hydrogen shell.

## Teaching tips

- **Start with helium**: Most students know helium-4 becomes superfluid. Position hydrogen as "the same physics, but in a molecule."
- **Use the spinning methane as the protagonist**: "A tiny methane molecule spinning inside a hydrogen shell" is a vivid mental image that carries the whole measurement principle.
- **Highlight the 20-year struggle**: This was not a quick result — it took two decades of technical development. This humanizes the science and shows why the freezing-point barrier was so hard to circumvent.
- **Connect to applications**: Mention that hydrogen storage/transport for clean energy could benefit from understanding hydrogen's quantum fluid behavior.
